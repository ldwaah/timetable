Position on direct match and suitable alternative employment

FC’s position on direct match and suitability (ACAS-aligned)

FC recognises that some elements of the FC's current role overlap with the proposed role, particularly administrative and coordination tasks. The FC's concern is not that there is no overlap, but that the proposed structure changes reporting lines, who signs off work, and practical expectations attached to those functions.

Meaningful consultation and ability to assess suitability

To assess whether a proposed role is suitable, FC needs enough practical information about how it would work day to day, not only how duties are described in a job description.

At the 18 May consultation meeting, several important practical points remained undefined. The ROH follow-up letter of 20 May 2026 records that workload allocation, how work would be shared across the team, helpdesk coverage between 8am and 6pm, expectations for part-time staff, and wider team arrangements would be discussed once the new manager is in post (expected mid to late June). Although FC works four days per week, how much office attendance would be required was similarly left for the new manager to discuss.

FC was asked to assess suitability and make decisions about the FC's employment, including whether to apply for voluntary redundancy, before these terms could be clarified. The voluntary redundancy deadline passed while workload split, helpdesk expectations, office attendance, and how shared work would be allocated remained unconfirmed.

FC’s concern is not resistance to change. The FC has continued to engage constructively in consultation. The concern is that the FC could not assess how the role would operate day to day before being asked to treat it as a predetermined direct match, and before key employment decisions were required.

The restructure is wide-ranging. The role should not be treated as an automatic direct match without fuller assessment. While there may be some overlap in duties, the proposed role appears to change the scope, structure, expectations, and practical operation of FC’s role. On that basis, it should be treated as proposed suitable alternative employment requiring a proper suitability assessment.

Moving operational expectations

  • The proposed E&F Support Officer role shifts emphasis toward helpdesk, reactive support, and administrative processing rather than coordinator-level coordination.
  • Reporting into a Support Manager with integrated team working changes who sets priorities, who signs off work, and how independently responsibilities are held.
  • ROH described the new role as an amalgamation of three roles. That does not show FC’s coordinator post is an automatic direct match without fuller assessment.

Hybrid working and whether the role is workable

  • FC works part-time (four days), hybrid (two office, two home), with arrangements structured around childcare.
  • At the start of consultation, flexible working was said to remain unchanged. Later, office attendance was said to be for the new manager to decide.
  • Expectations on office-based working may have been communicated more clearly to others than to FC, despite management knowing the FC's hybrid pattern and childcare commitments.

Practical concerns and consultation timing

  • Helpdesk coverage between 8am and 6pm was raised at consultation, but how that would work for part-time staff remains unclear and was to be discussed with the new manager.
  • Workload split, sickness cover, office attendance, and whether staff might be expected outside contracted days were deferred until a new manager is in post (mid to late June).
  • FC could not assess whether the role would work day to day before the voluntary redundancy deadline while these arrangements were still TBC.

Overlap in duties already performed

  • FC recognises overlap in access control coordination, contractor liaison, purchase orders, SharePoint maintenance, and coordination tasks.
  • The FC's concern is how those functions would work in practice: integrated team working, escalation to Manager, and different sign-off, not mere relabelling of duties the FC already performs.

Consultation quality

  • FC remains concerned that meaningful consultation would allow employees to assess suitability with enough practical detail before being asked to treat the role as a predetermined match.
  • Key questions on helpdesk hours, office attendance, workload, and team structure could not be answered at the 18 May meeting and remain open.

Conclusion. The proposed role may contain some familiar duties, but the extent of restructuring means it cannot reasonably be treated as an automatic direct match without fuller assessment. It appears to be a materially changed or merged role. It should therefore be treated as proposed suitable alternative employment, subject to proper consultation, written clarification, objective comparison, and consideration of FC’s circumstances.

Throughout consultation, FC has continued to engage constructively, raise practical questions, and consider the proposal in good faith. The FC's concerns relate to how the proposed role would work in practice, rather than resistance to organisational change itself.
